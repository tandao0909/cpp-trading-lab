## Reality Check

### The Competition

Top firms receive **10,000+ applications** for 20-50 positions per year. Most applicants have degrees from target schools; half have graduate degrees. You are competing against IMO medalists, Putnam Fellows, and published researchers.

### Degree Requirements

| Role | Minimum | Preferred |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Trader | Bachelor's from target school | -- |
| Developer | Bachelor's from target school | -- |
| Researcher | Master's | PhD |

Your school brand matters significantly for your first job.

### Work Requirements

- Expect **60-80 hour weeks** during critical periods
- High-pressure environment with immediate, daily feedback
- Poor performers are managed out quickly
- Burnout rate is high across all roles

---

## Target Schools

### US Undergraduate
MIT, Harvard, Princeton, Stanford, Yale, Columbia, UChicago, Caltech, Berkeley, CMU, Cornell, UPenn, Michigan, NYU

### US Graduate
All of the above, plus: Baruch MFE, UCLA MFE, Princeton MFin, CMU MSCF, NYU Tandon FRE, Columbia MFE, Chicago MSFM, MIT MFin

### International
Cambridge, Oxford, Imperial, ETH Zurich, EPFL, ENS Paris, Tsinghua, Peking University, IIT (India), Toronto, Waterloo

### Not at a Target School?

Transfer before junior year if possible. Otherwise, build an exceptional track record: publications, competition wins, a strong GitHub, and internships at name-brand tech firms. The path exists, but it requires top 1% credentials everywhere else.

## Quantitative Trader

Design and execute systematic trading strategies using statistical and mathematical models. Performance is directly tied to PnL.

### Minimum Requirements
- Bachelor's in math, physics, CS, or engineering from a target school
- GPA above 3.7
- Strong Python and C++
- Proven ability to think probabilistically under pressure
- Previous internship preferred

### Compensation

| Stage | Total Comp |
|-------|-----------|
| Year 1 | $200k-$350k |
| Year 3+ | $400k-$800k |
| Senior | $1M-$5M+ |

Bonus is heavily performance-based. Bad years mean base salary only.

---

### Coursework

#### Mathematics *(Critical)*
Multivariable Calculus, Linear Algebra, Differential Equations, Real Analysis, Probability Theory, Stochastic Processes, Optimization, Numerical Methods

#### Statistics *(Critical)*
Mathematical Statistics, Time Series Analysis, Bayesian Statistics, Statistical Inference, Regression Analysis, Econometrics

#### Computer Science *(Critical)*
Data Structures, Algorithms, OOP, Databases, Machine Learning, Systems Programming

#### Finance *(Recommended)*
Derivatives Pricing, Portfolio Theory, Market Microstructure, Financial Econometrics.

> Take these if offered, but technical skills matter more than finance theory.

---

### Technical Skills

**Programming**
- Python: Pandas, NumPy, scikit-learn, matplotlib. Able to write 1000+ line backtesting frameworks
- C++: STL, memory management, optimization. Implement low-latency data structures
- SQL: Complex queries, joins, window functions. Query market data efficiently

**Statistics and Probability**
- Calculate expected values and probabilities mentally
- Understand bias-variance tradeoff; know common distributions cold
- Apply hypothesis testing correctly; recognize when models overfit
- Work confidently with time series data

**Market Knowledge**
- Understand order types, execution, and market microstructure basics
- Read financial news daily
- Know how different asset classes trade and who the major participants are

---

### Projects to Build

**1. Trading Strategy Backtester**
Build from scratch in Python. Include transaction costs, slippage, and realistic fills. Calculate Sharpe ratio, max drawdown, and other metrics. Put on GitHub with full documentation.

**2. Market Data Analysis**
Download historical data, clean tick data, calculate volatility surfaces, analyze order book dynamics. Document findings in Jupyter notebooks.

**3. Options Pricing Tool**
Implement Black-Scholes and binomial tree models. Calculate Greeks numerically. Build a Monte Carlo pricer. Compare results across methods with visualizations.

---

### Critical Soft Skills

- **Decision Making Under Pressure** -- Stay calm when positions move against you. Cut losses quickly. Trust your models but override when necessary.
- **Communication** -- Explain complex strategies to non-technical stakeholders. Defend ideas with data. Write clear documentation.
- **Competitive Drive** -- Handle losses without an emotional spiral. Stay disciplined when ahead.

---

### Where to Apply

**Top Tier (Most Selective)**
Jane Street, Hudson River Trading, Citadel Securities, Tower Research, Two Sigma, Jump Trading, DE Shaw, Optiver, IMC Trading, Virtu Financial

**Strong Mid-Tier**
SIG, Akuna Capital, DRW, Old Mission, Wolverine, Flow Traders, Five Rings, GTS, Belvedere Trading, Radix Trading

**Hedge Funds with Quant Trading**
Renaissance Technologies, Millennium, Citadel (HF side), Point72, AQR, Bridgewater, PDT Partners, Balyasny

> Apply broadly across all tiers. Most positions are filled through internship conversion. Start applying **July-August** for the following summer.

---

### Interview Process

| Round | Format | Elimination Rate |
|-------|--------|-----------------|
| Phone Screen | Mental math, probability, brain teasers (30-45 min) | ~50% |
| Technical Rounds (2-4) | Probability, market making games, coding, strategy design | High |
| Final On-Site (4-6 interviews) | Technical + behavioral, trading simulations, case studies | 80-90% |

---

### Interview Preparation

**Books to Master**
- *Heard on the Street* -- Timothy Crack
- *A Practical Guide to Quantitative Finance Interviews* -- Xinfeng Zhou
- *Fifty Challenging Problems in Probability* -- Frederick Mosteller
- *Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives* -- John Hull (reference)

**Practice Requirements**
- Solve 500+ probability problems before interviewing
- Practice mental math daily until fast
- Complete 200+ LeetCode medium problems
- Simulate trading games with friends; time yourself on everything

**Common Mistakes**
- Giving up on problems too quickly
- Not showing work for mental calculations
- Memorizing answers without understanding
- Weak explanation of thought process

---

### Undergraduate Timeline

| Year | Focus |
|------|-------|
| Freshman | Calculus, linear algebra, intro CS. Learn Python. Maintain 3.8+ GPA. |
| Sophomore | Probability, statistics, data structures. Apply to trading competitions and internships. |
| Junior | Stochastic processes, time series, algorithms. Complete internship. Apply everywhere in July-August. **Critical recruiting year.** |
| Senior | Convert internship or interview again. Most competitive roles filled by October. Have backups ready. |

## Quantitative Developer

Build and maintain the systems that support quantitative trading and research. Bridge the gap between researchers and production. Performance is measured on code quality and delivery speed.

### Minimum Requirements
- Bachelor's in CS, math, or engineering from a target school
- GPA above 3.5
- Strong C++ and Python
- Data structures, algorithms, and system design knowledge
- Finance knowledge helpful but not required initially

### Compensation

| Stage | Total Comp |
|-------|-----------|
| Year 1 | $150k-$300k |
| Year 3+ | $250k-$500k |
| Senior | $400k-$800k |

More stable than trading, but lower ceiling.

---

### Coursework

#### Computer Science *(Critical)*
Data Structures, Algorithms, Operating Systems, Computer Architecture, Databases, Distributed Systems, Networks, Compilers, Software Engineering

#### Mathematics *(Critical)*
Calculus through multivariable, Linear Algebra, Discrete Math, Probability, Statistics, Numerical Methods, Optimization

#### Advanced Electives *(Recommended)*
High-Performance Computing, Parallel Programming, Real-Time Systems, ML Systems, Database Internals, Network Programming

#### Finance *(Optional)*
Derivatives, Market Microstructure, Quantitative Finance. Learn finance on the job -- technical skills take priority.

---

### Technical Skills

**Programming Languages**
- C++ (C++14/17/20): templates, STL, memory management, high-performance code, compiler optimization
- Python: scientific stack, async programming, profiling, research tooling
- Java, Rust, or Go depending on firm; SQL for data access

**System Design**
- Design scalable distributed systems with latency requirements in mind
- Know trade-offs between consistency and availability
- Handle high-throughput data streams and fault tolerance

**Performance Optimization**
- Profile code to find bottlenecks
- Optimize memory access patterns, reduce cache misses
- Understand CPU architecture; write lock-free concurrent code

---

### Projects to Build

**1. Order Book Implementation**
Build a limit order book in C++ with microsecond latency. Support all order types with price-time priority. Measure, optimize, and document the API clearly.

**2. Market Data Feed Handler**
Parse real market data protocols. Handle packet loss and retransmission at high message rates. Write efficiently to a database with real-time monitoring.

**3. Backtesting Framework**
Build a production-quality backtester in C++. Support multiple strategies, handle corporate actions, calculate accurate transaction costs, and make it fast enough for parameter sweeps.

---

### Critical Soft Skills

- **Attention to Detail** -- Bugs in trading systems cost millions. Test thoroughly; document assumptions; handle every edge case.
- **Communication with Non-Technical Staff** -- Translate business logic into code; push back on infeasible requests with data.
- **Working Under Pressure** -- Fix production issues quickly while markets are open. Prioritize correctly during incidents.

---

### Where to Apply

**Top Trading Firms**
Jane Street, Hudson River Trading, Citadel Securities, Jump Trading, Tower Research, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Optiver, IMC Trading, SIG

**Quantitative Hedge Funds**
Renaissance Technologies, Millennium, Citadel, Point72, AQR, Bridgewater, PDT Partners, WorldQuant, Balyasny

**Banks with Strong Quant Groups**
Goldman Sachs Strats, JP Morgan QR, Morgan Stanley Strats, Barclays Quantitative Analytics
*(Lower pay than trading firms, but good training)*

> More positions available than in trading. Strong developers are always in demand and can transition internally to research later.

---

### Interview Process

| Round | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Coding (3-5 rounds) | LeetCode hard problems, focus on algorithms and data structures. Bug-free C++ expected. |
| System Design | Design a trading system or data pipeline. Discuss latency, fault tolerance, and scaling. |
| Domain Knowledge | Basic probability, financial instruments, order book concepts. |

---

### Interview Preparation

- Complete **300+ LeetCode problems** (medium and hard); master all common patterns
- Practice writing C++ without an IDE; time yourself strictly
- Read *Designing Data-Intensive Applications* -- Martin Kleppmann
- Read *Effective Modern C++* -- Scott Meyers; know move semantics and STL internals deeply

---

### Undergraduate Timeline

| Year | Focus |
|------|-------|
| Freshman | Intro CS, calculus. Learn C++ and Python. Start building projects. |
| Sophomore | Data structures, algorithms, systems courses. Start LeetCode. Apply broadly for internships. |
| Junior | Complete core CS requirements. Internship at trading firm or top tech company. Apply to all targets. **Most important recruiting year.** |
| Senior | Finish degree. Convert internship or interview again. Consider grad school if research interests develop. |

## Quantitative Researcher

Develop mathematical models for pricing, risk management, or alpha generation. Conduct original research on market behavior. Requires deep theoretical knowledge combined with practical implementation skills.

### Minimum Requirements
- Master's degree in a quantitative field from a target school *(minimum)*
- PhD strongly preferred for most positions
- GPA above 3.7
- Strong publication record helpful
- Exceptional mathematical ability
- Python and C++ proficiency
- Ability to conduct independent research

### Compensation

| Stage | Total Comp |
|-------|-----------|
| Year 1 | $150k-$400k |
| Year 3+ | $300k-$700k |
| Senior | $500k-$2M+ |

Performance-based at hedge funds; more stable at banks.

---

### Undergraduate Foundation

#### Core Mathematics
Real Analysis, Complex Analysis, Measure Theory, Linear Algebra, Abstract Algebra, Differential Equations, Topology, Functional Analysis

#### Probability and Statistics
Probability Theory, Mathematical Statistics, Stochastic Processes, Stochastic Calculus, Time Series Analysis, Martingale Theory, Measure-Theoretic Probability

#### Applied Mathematics
Numerical Analysis, Optimization, PDEs, Monte Carlo Methods, Computational Mathematics, Scientific Computing

#### Programming
Data Structures, Algorithms, Machine Learning, Statistical Computing. Strong Python and C++.

---

### Graduate Education Path

**Master's Programs** *(Required Minimum)*
Financial Engineering, Computational Finance, Applied Mathematics, Statistics, CS, or Operations Research. Top programs: Baruch, CMU, Princeton, MIT, Columbia.

**PhD Programs** *(Strongly Recommended)*
Mathematics, Statistics, Physics, CS, Financial Economics, or Operations Research.
- PhD gives a significant advantage for research roles
- Choose an advisor with finance industry connections
- Publish papers during the program
- Strong dissertation topics: stochastic control, optimal execution, market microstructure, statistical arbitrage, ML for finance, volatility modeling, credit risk, high-dimensional statistics

---

### Technical Skills

**Mathematical Modeling**
Build models from first principles. Derive closed-form solutions when possible. Understand model assumptions deeply and know when they break down. Validate models rigorously.

**Statistical Methods**
Time series analysis, cointegration, factor models, regime-switching models, hidden Markov models, Kalman filtering, machine learning, hypothesis testing, resampling, cross-validation

**Programming for Research**
- Python: Pandas, NumPy, SciPy, scikit-learn, TensorFlow -- for rapid prototyping
- C++: for production implementation
- R or MATLAB: for statistical/numerical work
- Git: always

---

### Research Skills

- **Literature Review** -- Read papers constantly. Know the seminal works in your area. Follow leading researchers and attend academic conferences.
- **Experimental Design** -- Avoid look-ahead bias and survivorship bias. Use proper train-test splits. Account for transaction costs realistically.
- **Writing and Communication** -- Write clear research reports. Present findings to traders and PMs. Defend your methodology.

---

### Finance Knowledge

**Asset Pricing Theory**
CAPM, APT, factor models, Black-Scholes, local/stochastic volatility, term structure models, credit risk models

**Market Microstructure**
Order flow, bid-ask spread, price impact, liquidity provision, HFT mechanics, optimal execution, transaction cost analysis

**Risk Management**
VaR, CVaR, portfolio optimization, risk decomposition, Greeks, hedging strategies, stress testing

---

### Critical Soft Skills

- **Intellectual Honesty** -- Admit when models fail. Do not cherry-pick results. Distinguish correlation from causation. Avoid confirmation bias.
- **Persistence** -- Most research leads nowhere. Stay motivated during dry periods; try different approaches systematically.
- **Collaboration** -- Work with traders to understand their needs. Coordinate with developers on implementation. Share credit appropriately.

---

### Where to Apply

**Top Quantitative Hedge Funds**
Renaissance Technologies, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Citadel, Millennium, AQR, PDT Partners, WorldQuant, Point72, Bridgewater

**Trading Firms (Research Teams)**
Jane Street, Hudson River Trading, Jump Trading, Tower Research, Optiver, IMC Trading, SIG, DRW, Akuna Capital

**Investment Banks**
Goldman Sachs QIS, JP Morgan QR, Morgan Stanley QAD, Barclays Quantitative Analytics
*(More structured environment, better work-life balance, strong training programs -- good starting point)*

> PhD candidates are recruited during their final year. Publications and strong academic references are critical. Competition is extremely intense.

## Common Application Mistakes

| Mistake | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Weak technical foundation** | Gaps in core math or programming show immediately in interviews. Memorizing answers without understanding fails every time. |
| **Poor school choice** | Attending a non-target school without exceptional credentials. Choosing a graduate program based on location over reputation. |
| **Late preparation** | Starting interview prep too late. Missing internship recruitment cycles. Apply **early** and prepare **thoroughly**. |
| **Resume problems** | Irrelevant experience, typos, vague project descriptions, no quantified achievements, longer than one page. |
| **Lack of genuine interest** | Inability to explain what the firm does or discuss markets intelligently. Interviewers detect this immediately. |
| **Arrogance** | Not admitting when you don't know something. Arguing with interviewers. Culture fit matters as much as technical skills. |

### Career Fairs
Attend all finance and tech career fairs. Prepare specific questions for each firm. Follow up with everyone you meet.

### Conferences and Competitions
Attend quantitative finance conferences. Win math and programming competitions. Meeting recruiters at these events provides strong signal.

- Bonus is tied to performance; it can be **zero** in bad years

## Job Security

### Performance Expectations
- **Traders** -- Measured on PnL daily; poor performance leads to quick termination
- **Researchers** -- Expected to produce results within 6-12 months
- **Developers** -- Judged on code quality and delivery speed

### Turnover
**30-40%** of new hires leave within 2 years, due to performance exits, burnout, or market downturns.

### How to Build Stability
Deliver consistent strong performance. Develop specialized expertise. Build good relationships with your managers. Save money aggressively early.

### US Work Authorization
- H-1B visa required for most positions; firms sponsor qualified candidates
- OPT allows **3 years** for STEM degrees -- start the visa process early

### Regional Markets
London, Hong Kong, and Singapore have strong quant markets. Compensation levels and visa requirements differ by region.

- Target firms with a strong H-1B sponsorship history

## Women in Quantitative Finance

Industry remains male-dominated -- approximately 15-20% of quants are women, with higher representation in research than trading. Firms are actively recruiting women, but culture varies significantly by firm.

- Connect with Women in Quantitative Finance and firm-specific networks
- Find mentors and allies early
- Research firm culture before accepting an offer

## Self-Assessment

Before committing to this path, answer these honestly.

### Are You Ready for Quant Trader?
- [ ] Can you solve probability problems under time pressure?
- [ ] Do you stay calm when losing money?
- [ ] Can you make quick decisions with incomplete information?
- [ ] Are you genuinely interested in markets?
- [ ] Can you handle intense daily competition?

### Are You Ready for Quant Developer?
- [ ] Can you write production-quality code?
- [ ] Do you care deeply about performance and correctness?
- [ ] Can you debug complex systems?
- [ ] Can you communicate clearly with non-technical people?

### Are You Ready for Quant Researcher?
- [ ] Do you enjoy mathematical research?
- [ ] Can you handle months of failed experiments?
- [ ] Do you read academic papers regularly?
- [ ] Are you intellectually honest about results?

### General
- [ ] Can you work 60+ hours weekly?
- [ ] Are you comfortable with performance pressure?
- [ ] Can you commit to years of preparation?

> If most answers are **no**, reconsider this path. These roles demand a specific temperament. It is better to find the right fit than to force the wrong one.

### Backup Options
- **Finance-adjacent**: Risk management, fintech data science, portfolio management, actuarial work
- **Tech**: Software engineering or ML at FAANG -- often better work-life balance; can lateral back later
- **Academia**: Research professor, government labs, think tanks -- lower pay but intellectual freedom

## Mental Health

### Stress Management
High-pressure environments cause real stress. Exercise regularly, maintain relationships outside work, and protect your sleep -- burnout is common and serious.

### Imposter Syndrome
Common among high achievers. Everyone feels inadequate sometimes. Focus on continuous improvement; ask for help when needed; therapy can help.

### When to Leave
No amount of money is worth consistently damaging your mental health. Recognize warning signs early. Have an exit plan. Many successful people leave finance and thrive.

## Set Realistic Expectations

- **Less than 5%** of applicants receive offers at top firms

- Multiple application cycles are common; luck plays a role
- Do not take rejection personally

| Path | Total Years |
|------|------------|

## Succeeding in Your First Year

- **Learn constantly** -- Absorb everything from senior colleagues. Understand firm-specific tools and systems deeply.
- **Deliver results** -- Meet deadlines. Fix mistakes quickly. Exceed expectations when possible. Build a reputation as a reliable performer.
- **Build relationships** -- Find mentors. Be helpful. Understand the firm's politics. Maintain professionalism always.

### Years 2-5
- Develop specialization in a specific asset class or technique
- Evaluate opportunities at other firms; know your market value
- Save aggressively; do not inflate your lifestyle too quickly

## Internship Strategy

### Sophomore Summer
Any technical internship is valuable. Target tech companies if no quant finance offers are available. Focus on building programming skills and learning a professional environment.

### Junior Summer
This is the **critical** summer. Target trading firms and hedge funds. Perform exceptionally -- most full-time offers come from internship conversion. Build genuine relationships with your team.

### No Internship?
- Build exceptional projects independently
- Contribute to open source
- Take summer courses in advanced topics
- Do research with a professor

## Evaluating Job Offers

### What to Look For Beyond Salary
- Learning opportunities and quality of mentorship
- Team quality and firm culture
- Role responsibilities and growth trajectory
- Work-life balance and long-term career path

### Red Flags
- Extremely high turnover
- Poor Glassdoor reviews from technical staff
- Vague job descriptions
- Pressure to accept quickly
- Low emphasis on training
- Unclear performance metrics

### Questions to Ask
- What does success look like in the first year?
- Who will I work with daily?
- What training is provided?
- How is performance measured?
- What are typical career paths here?
- Can I speak with recent hires?
